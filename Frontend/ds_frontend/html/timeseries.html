TIME SERIES FORECASTING

1. What is the main purpose of the ARIMA model in time series forecasting?

Correct Option: B) To model and forecast time series data by capturing temporal dependencies

Solution and Explanation:
ARIMA is used to model and forecast time series data by capturing autoregressive (AR) and moving average (MA) dependencies, along with differencing (I) to make the data stationary.

**Key Components of ARIMA**:
1. Autoregressive (AR) term.
2. Integrated (I) term (for differencing).
3. Moving Average (MA) term.




2. What does the "I" term in ARIMA stand for?

Correct Option: B) Integrated (for differencing the time series to make it stationary)

Solution and Explanation:
The "I" term in ARIMA refers to "Integration," which is used to make the time series data stationary by differencing the series to remove trends or seasonal components.

**Steps for Stationarity**:
1. Differencing the time series.
2. Checking for stationarity with tests like ADF.




3. Which of the following is a key assumption in ARIMA modeling?

Correct Option: C) The time series should be stationary

Solution and Explanation:
One of the main assumptions of ARIMA is that the time series should be stationary. If the series is not stationary, differencing is applied to make it stationary.

**Stationarity Requirements**:
1. Constant mean.
2. Constant variance.
3. Constant autocovariance.




4. Which of the following is used to determine the optimal order of AR and MA terms in an ARIMA model?

Correct Option: B) ACF and PACF plots

Solution and Explanation:
The autocorrelation function (ACF) and partial autocorrelation function (PACF) plots are used to determine the appropriate orders for the AR and MA terms of the ARIMA model.

**Using ACF and PACF**:
1. ACF is used for determining the MA term.
2. PACF is used for determining the AR term.




5. What does the "AR" term in ARIMA represent?

Correct Option: B) Autoregressive (the relationship between the current observation and its previous values)

Solution and Explanation:
The "AR" term in ARIMA refers to the autoregressive component, which captures the dependency between an observation and a number of lagged observations.

**Autoregressive Process**:
1. Models the relationship between a time series and its past values.
2. Uses lagged values of the series.




6. What is the key difference between ARIMA and SARIMA models?

**SARIMA Model Components**:
1. Seasonal AR (AutoRegressive).

3. Seasonal MA (Moving Average).




7. In SARIMA, what does the "S" in the model acronym represent?

Solution and Explanation:
The "S" in SARIMA stands for "Seasonal," indicating that the model takes seasonal patterns into account, in addition to the regular autoregressive, integrated, and moving average components.

**Seasonal Effects**:
1. Repeating patterns at regular intervals.
2. Account for cyclical trends, such as yearly, quarterly, or monthly patterns.




8. Which of the following is true about seasonal differencing in SARIMA?

Correct Option: C) Seasonal differencing helps to remove seasonal trends by subtracting the value of a previous season from the current value

Solution and Explanation:
Seasonal differencing is used to handle seasonality in time series. It involves subtracting the value of the previous season (e.g., one year ago) from the current value to remove seasonal trends.

**Seasonal Differencing Formula**:
\( Y_t = Y_t - Y_{t-s} \)
Where \( s \) is the length of the season (e.g., 12 for monthly data with yearly seasonality).

Solution and Explanation:

**Moving Average Process**:
1. Models the error term of the series.
2. Uses weighted average of past residual errors.

27. Which of the following is a key feature of GRU networks?

Correct Option: C) GRUs use update and reset gates to control the flow of information

Solution and Explanation:
GRUs use two gates—an update gate and a reset gate—unlike LSTMs, which use three gates (input, forget, and output). The update gate controls how much of the previous memory should be carried forward, and the reset gate decides how much of the past information to forget.

**GRU Gates**:
1. Update gate (controls how much of the past state is carried forward).
2. Reset gate (decides how much past information to forget).

**Vanishing Gradient Problem**:
1. Occurs in traditional RNNs during training with long sequences.
2. Both GRUs and LSTMs solve this issue by using gates to control information flow.

36. What is the primary purpose of the Seasonal Decomposition of Time Series (STL)?

**STL Components**:
1. Trend: Long-term movements in the data.
2. Seasonal: Regular patterns that repeat over time.
3. Residual: Random noise or irregular components.

Solution and Explanation:
STL uses LOESS (Locally Estimated Scatterplot Smoothing) to perform the seasonal decomposition. LOESS is a non-parametric method that fits a series of local polynomials to data points to smooth out trends and seasonal patterns.

**STL and LOESS**:
1. LOESS helps in smoothing the trend and seasonal components.
2. It is useful in cases where data shows irregular patterns.

**Additive vs. Multiplicative**:

47. Which method is commonly used in Time Series Cross-Validation to split the data?

Correct Option: C) Rolling or Expanding Window

Solution and Explanation:
The most common methods for splitting the data in Time Series Cross-Validation are the Rolling Window and Expanding Window approaches. In Rolling Window, the training and test sets are shifted forward in time. In Expanding Window, the training set expands as the time moves forward, while the test set remains the same size.

**Methods for Splitting Data**:
1. Rolling Window: Keeps the size of the training set fixed.
2. Expanding Window: Expands the training set over time.
